\section{Worked example}

We build the design up rather than show it whole. Each rung adds one source of difficulty and turns the threat it introduces into a lesson, so the reader can see what a corpus difference-in-differences identifies and where it stops. The phenomenon throughout is the feminization of profession nouns under language policy. No rung is offered as a settled finding about feminization; each is a vehicle for the method.

\subsection{Rung 1: a two-group baseline}

The cleanest start is a pair of varieties that share a language and differ in policy. French-speaking Switzerland endorsed feminine profession nouns early: the Swiss Federal Chancellery's 1991 report on non-sexist administrative language and \textcite{Moreau1991}'s professions dictionary. France makes the comparison, but not a pristine control. A 1986 circular under Prime Minister Fabius urged the same forms yet went largely unenforced, by the government's own later admission, until a second circular under Jospin in 1998 \citep{BurnettBonami2019}. We therefore treat 1986 as a near-null policy, take 1991--1997 as the window in which Switzerland is treated and France is not, and hold the 1998 French change back as a separate follow-up event.

The outcome is the proportion of profession and title nouns that take feminine marking when the referent is a woman, counted strategy-agnostically: any feminine article, agreement, or noun form rather than one favoured ending. The two varieties differ a little in preferred form, Switzerland tolerating \mention{-esse} where France resists feminine endings longer \citep{Dawes2003}, but sharing the language spares the comparison the gulf between French, German, and Italian. Because that count assumes the forms are exchangeable signs of one change, we also report a form-specific estimate and read a divergence between the two as a warning, not a detail. We take female reference off independent cues, chiefly \mention{Madame} with a title, so the \mention{le}/\mention{la} alternation is identified without hand-coding.

Suisse romande's press is far smaller than France's, so the rarer titles land in thin cells. Partial pooling across outlets and titles within each variety steadies those sparse estimates, though for a two-period contrast the pre and post averaging already does most of that work, so the real payoff waits for the staggered design of rung 2 and its many group-time cells. The two varieties stay fixed effects so the contrast we are after is not pooled away, and the pooling stops short of the editing layer, which we keep on display.

Two cautions belong up front. The corpora are edited newspapers, so the design identifies a change in the edited written standard, not in spontaneous usage; use in the wider population stays a separate target, reached only through the lightly edited and spoken material added later.

Corpus frequency is also a population rate plus a composition term, so the usual parallel-trends assumption now has to hold twice over: the corpus adds parallel trends in the composition term to the standard requirement of parallel trends in the rate, it does not replace it. The response to that second requirement is to reweight to a fixed composition, but only on margins the policy does not itself move: a profession or topic mix that the endorsement shifts is a mediator, and adjusting it would subtract part of the effect, not just the bias.

The two groups buy one sharp question, with a trap inside it. If the 1991 endorsement changed usage, Suisse romande should accelerate away from France after 1991 while the two ran together before. But an endorsement often ratifies a change already under way, so a pre-1991 divergence would mean 1991 is a waypoint on a curve, not its onset, and the later gap is no policy effect. We therefore check the pre-1991 slopes first and ask whether the adoption rate changes at 1991, not merely whether a gap opens. If instead both series rise together in calendar time throughout, the driver is a shared francophone shift in norms and the design has caught the wave, not the intervention.

The threat this rung introduces is interference. Suisse romande reads French media, and both draw on the same wire service, so the control is not sealed off from the treated variety, and we must say which effect we want: the Swiss policy's direct effect, net of what crosses the border, or its total effect including diffusion. We make Agence France-Presse share and French-media dependence exposure variables rather than nuisances to delete, hold shared-wire copy aside for a separate estimate, and report how much of each series it carries. A jump that lives only in shared material is diffusion, not a Swiss policy effect.

We also run the procedure on deliberately broken data, shuffling the treatment year to random dates and permuting the variety labels, to confirm it finds nothing where nothing should be. With only two varieties this is a falsification and calibration check, not powered inference: a clean panel offers no real randomization, so the permuted contrasts tell us the size of jump the pipeline manufactures by chance, against which the observed contrast is read. We do not present a permutation $p$-value as though the two varieties had been randomly assigned \citep{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}.

These checks earn their keep only if they are allowed to change the conclusion, so we fix in advance what each verdict permits:
\begin{itemize}
\item a divergence at 1991 that survives the composition-margin, shared-wire, and pre-1991-slope checks, with a breakdown value larger than any plausible ideology shift, licenses a \emph{bounded} estimate of a policy effect on the edited written standard;
\item a divergence that leans on an ambiguous check, or a breakdown value near a plausible ideology shift, licenses only a \emph{descriptive} reading;
\item calendar-time co-movement with no divergence at 1991 is read as the shared wave rather than the policy, an identified result in its own right;
\item a pre-1991 divergence, an effect carried by shared-wire or single-outlet material, or a plausible ideology shift that overturns the estimate, forces \emph{not identified}, which we report rather than call the result robust.
\end{itemize}
A population claim is licensed by none of these on its own; it waits for the spoken and lightly edited series.

Rung 1 thus carries the whole apparatus in miniature, a stated estimand, a falsifiable parallel-trends assumption, an interference diagnostic, a randomization check, and an explicit rule for what each may conclude, on the cleanest data the phenomenon offers. The rungs that follow keep the apparatus and the rule and add the complications cross-variety work cannot avoid.

\subsection{Rung 2: scaling to staggered adoption}

Adding varieties looks like a free improvement and is not. Québec endorsed feminine titles in 1979 and Belgium by décret in 1993; with Suisse romande (1991) and France (1998) that is four polities adopting at four dates. Treatment timing now varies, so the two-group contrast gives way to staggered estimators: we read group-time effects against not-yet-treated varieties \citep{CallawaySantAnna2021}, after the standard warning that a pooled two-way fixed-effects estimate is a weighted average of two-by-two contrasts whose weights can go negative \citep{GoodmanBacon2021,deChaisemartin2020}, and we use event-study estimators robust to that contamination \citep{SunAbraham2021,BorusyakJaravelSpiess2024}.

But four polities are not four independent experiments, and this rung exists to show why. The varieties are now whole countries, each with its own politics, press economy, and media regime that the two-group baseline never had to hold apart. The forms diverge as well: Québec writes \mention{auteure}, Belgium leans epicene, France resists, so rung 1's strategy-agnostic count now rests on treating these as exchangeable signs of one change, which we test rather than assume \citep{Dawes2003}.

The deep cost is that the staggering is not exogenous. A polity legislates feminine titles about when its gender politics have already moved, and that same movement shifts usage, so the not-yet-treated varieties are not resting on a flat counterfactual but riding the continental wave that constitutes the effect. The control is contaminated in the one dimension the design needs clean, and no estimator repairs it. \textcite{RambachanRoth2023} can bound how large a trends violation must be to overturn a result, but it cannot manufacture exogeneity where treatment timing is selected on the outcome's own trajectory; and with four or five clusters the asymptotics fail, leaving cluster-robust intervals anti-conservative and the permutation set tiny \citep{BertrandDufloMullainathan2004}.

What the staggering does buy is a sharper form of rung 1's question. If the policies move usage, the jumps should track each polity's own date; if a shared wave moves it, they should track calendar time whatever the date. France supplies the internal placebo: its 1986 circular went unenforced, on the government's own later admission, and should show no jump where 1998 does, so a design that reports an effect at 1986 is reading something other than the policy \citep{BurnettBonami2019,Fujimura2005}. It is a negative control for one date, not a warrant for the parallel trends the other varieties still require.

The decision ladder carries over unchanged in form, with the staggered diagnostics slotted in. A cross-variety divergence aligned to policy dates, surviving the composition, shared-wire, and pre-trend checks, with breakdown values beyond a plausible wave, licenses a bounded estimate; calendar-time alignment is read as the wave; a failed France placebo, contamination revealed by the decomposition, or a plausible wave that overturns the estimate forces \emph{not identified}. More varieties raise the bar for a clean claim at least as often as they lower it.

\subsection{What the simulations show, and where they break}

A corpus never reveals the population rate, so the one place we can check that the design recovers the right answer is a simulation where we set it. On data we control, the claim ladder routes each canonical case to the verdict it should, and reweighting recovers the true effect when a composition shift rides an observed stratum: a naive contrast of $+0.13$ on a pure artifact returns to zero once the mix is held fixed. That is a consistency check, not evidence the diagnostics have power on messy data.

So we map the failures, recording how often the ladder reaches the wrong verdict as each threat is turned up. Measured composition is caught or corrected, but drift the strata do not capture is invisible, and the false-positive rate climbs past half and on to a near-certainty as the hidden share grows. Thin cells destroy power: a small effect in a small variety is missed more than nine times in ten. Interference is caught when exposure is measured well and leaks false positives when it is not.

And selection into treatment timing, a control on its own trajectory, is observationally identical to a real effect in a two-group design, an irreducible false positive that only the cross-variety calendar-time test of rung 2 can catch.

Read together, the simulations are sanity checks and a failure map, not a certificate that the method works. They mark the conditions under which a corpus difference-in-differences earns a bounded claim, and the conditions under which the honest verdict is descriptive or not identified.
