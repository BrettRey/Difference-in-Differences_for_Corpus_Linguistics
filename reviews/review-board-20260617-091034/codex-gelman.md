1. The plan’s core idea is to translate DiD from an econometric design label into a corpus-linguistic checklist for causal claims about linguistic change after shocks.

2. Strengths

- The six core questions are the right kind of discipline: they force the paper to name the treatment, outcome, comparison, threat, and diagnostic rather than waving at “before/after change.”
- The corpus-specific threat list is promising because it treats composition, topic, author turnover, and measurement drift as identification problems, not mere data-cleaning nuisances.
- The planned diagnostics section could be the paper’s real contribution if it shows how a corpus linguist would falsify or weaken a DiD story before estimating effects.

3. Weaknesses / risks

- The missing premise is why a DiD estimand is the right target at all. A binary treated/control, pre/post contrast can be a lossy summary of a richer time series; the plan needs to justify that loss rather than inherit DiD as the genre.
- The comparison corpus risks becoming decorative. The paper must say what variation in the comparison series would actually pin down the counterfactual trajectory, and what observed failures would make the comparison unusable.
- “Modern DiD choices” could distract from the harder problem. Staggered-adoption estimators and heterogeneity-robust machinery are secondary if the units, timing, and corpus-composition process do not support the identifying contrast.

4. DiD vs. multilevel model of the whole corpus time series

I would start from a multilevel time-series model: outcomes varying by corpus, register, author, topic, document source, time, and possibly linguistic item, with partial pooling and explicit uncertainty about trends and shocks. DiD buys a simple causal contrast and a transparent negative-control logic: if the treated corpus shifts relative to a comparison corpus at the shock, that is easier to explain to applied linguists than a large hierarchical model. But it costs a lot: it collapses time, encourages a sharp before/after fiction, hides gradual diffusion, and can sell the comparison series as a counterfactual without modeling why it should be one. The paper should frame DiD as a restricted, design-based summary that is sometimes useful, not as the default causal tool. The honest question is: does the DiD contrast approximate what a serious model of the whole series would learn, or does it replace that model with a convenient slogan?

5. Single question before drafting

What is the worked example in which the comparison corpus is not merely plausible-looking, but demonstrably constrains the counterfactual trajectory that the treated corpus would have followed without the shock?

6. Verdict

Promising but needs reframing: the foundation is useful, but the paper must justify DiD against fuller time-series modeling and make the comparison corpus earn its identifying role.
